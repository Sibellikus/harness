# UTP Requirements

This skill turns raw UTP material into a requirements contract for a provable business offer. It does not start from slogans. It starts from the customer's current operating process and proves what the product replaces, why the replacement is better, and how the result is measured.

Core framing:

```text
how the process works now -> which process element the product replaces -> why this is better -> what measurable business result appears
```

Do not write polished copy unless the user explicitly asks for copy. First make the requirements, checks, contradictions, and missing facts explicit.

## When To Use

Use this skill for:

- extracting UTP requirements from a transcript, chat, brief, sales conversation, product notes, or draft;
- reviewing whether a UTP is provable through process replacement rather than generic benefit language;
- building a requirements table before writing landing, one-pager, pitch, demo, cold outreach, or sales-deck copy;
- turning vague claims like "saves time", "improves sales", "helps managers", or "gives control" into process-specific requirements and metrics;
- extracting objection-handling requirements from buyer distrust, manager resistance, CRM comparisons, adoption fears, and control-risk concerns.

Do not use this skill for generic product planning, feature prioritization, or implementation architecture unless the user is asking how those details support the UTP.

## Main Principle

The UTP must be based on a concrete comparison, not a marketing abstraction:

- current client process;
- pain, risk, delay, control gap, or cost in that process;
- what the product does instead of the employee, manager, or manual coordination step;
- why the product-controlled process is more predictable, visible, measurable, or result-oriented;
- which metric proves the change.

Reject or flag claims that cannot be tied to a process element and a measurement.

Do not let the UTP collapse into "we reduce preparation time" if the source material says the deeper value is process replacement, management control, and operational visibility.

## Required Analysis

### 1. Current Process Decomposition

Break the customer's current path from request to result into operational elements. Depending on the domain, examples include:

- receiving the request or signal;
- preparing for contact or action;
- analyzing customer, product, context, terms, or constraints;
- defining the next step;
- assigning or confirming the responsible person;
- controlling execution;
- recording the result;
- passing status to management;
- coordinating with adjacent departments;
- closing the deal, completing the work, or identifying the blocking problem.

For each element, ask what happens now, who owns it, where it breaks, and how the break affects money, speed, control, or quality.

The output should read like a process roadmap, not a feature list. Each row must show a before/after replacement inside one controlled business process.

### 2. Replacement Map

For every meaningful current-process element, state what the product replaces:

- manager's manual preparation;
- employee discipline or memory;
- ad hoc control by a manager;
- spreadsheet, chat, meeting, CRM note, or manual report;
- cross-department coordination;
- delayed status collection;
- subjective judgment without facts;
- unclear responsibility;
- untracked problem escalation.

The product can assist, automate, guide, coordinate, or expose status. Do not overstate full replacement when the human still decides, approves, negotiates, or performs the work.

### 3. Control Instead Of Micromanagement

Business buyers do not trust "AI for AI's sake". They trust a process if it is:

- understandable;
- predictable;
- controllable;
- assigned to a responsible owner;
- producing intermediate results;
- interruptible at the right point;
- backed by numbers, facts, and deviations.

The UTP must prove that the product does not remove control. It makes control simpler, earlier, and more transparent.

Also identify the current human-control dependency: what the team lead, head of sales, or owner currently knows because they understand individual employees' character, skill, experience, habits, and weaknesses. The UTP must show how the product preserves or improves that control without requiring constant personal supervision.

### 4. Regular Management Coverage

When the product claims management value, verify whether it covers the basic management cycle:

- reports;
- tasks;
- problems;
- goals;
- responsible owners;
- tools or suggested actions;
- execution control;
- status by deal, process, employee, department, or objective;
- daily management summary or live management picture.

If these are not real product capabilities, do not let them enter the UTP as promises. Mark them as missing capability, future vision, or open decision.

### 5. Cross-Department Process Scope

If the sale or business result depends on multiple teams, the UTP must not pretend the sales manager alone controls the outcome. Check whether the process includes:

- sales;
- logistics;
- quality control;
- warehouse;
- production;
- service;
- finance;
- other adjacent departments.

The UTP should show whether the product tracks only the salesperson or the whole process that affects money.

### 6. Metrics And Management Facts

The UTP must define the operational picture a manager gets. Minimum metric candidates:

- how many deals, requests, tasks, or processes are active;
- where the process is slowing down;
- which tasks are not closed;
- which problems block revenue or progress;
- which goals are on track or off track;
- who is responsible;
- where management intervention is needed;
- efficiency by employee, team, department, or process stage;
- daily dynamics and changes since the previous check.

Prefer live metrics or a daily management summary over vague "reporting someday".

### 7. Money-Linked Action Focus

The UTP should explain how the product keeps employees focused on the key actions that move the business result. It should separate money-moving actions from bureaucracy, manual coordination, and low-value operational noise.

Use this standard:

```text
The product guides employees through the key actions that move the deal, process, or money forward, while reducing manual coordination, status gathering, and operational noise.
```

Only keep this claim if the product actually prioritizes actions, exposes next steps, or ties tasks to outcomes.

### 8. Buyer Distrust And Objection Handling

Objections are requirements, not afterthoughts. For every major objection, extract the real fear behind it and answer through process design, control points, metrics, or proof.

Use this table:

```text
| Objection | Real fear | Product/process answer | Proof required | Bad answer to avoid |
|---|---|---|---|---|
```

Common objection classes:

- "The manager/team lead already knows how to run this" -> fear of losing context, intuition, and informal control.
- "Managers will not track the process correctly" -> fear that the product depends on employee discipline.
- "Business does not trust AI" -> fear of a black box; answer with visible steps, responsible owners, facts, and override/control points.
- "This is just CRM" -> fear of buying another heavy system; answer by showing which operating process is replaced and why CRM-like elements exist only to control that process.
- "Employees will resist extra reporting" -> fear of bureaucracy; answer by showing that the product reduces status chasing and focuses work on money-moving actions.
- "Numbers will be late or unreliable" -> fear that management still lacks the daily picture; answer with live metrics, daily summary, ownership, and deviation reporting.
- "Adjacent departments will block sales anyway" -> fear that sales-only tooling cannot affect the real revenue process; answer with cross-department tracking and responsibility boundaries.

Never answer objections with generic reassurance. The answer must name a product behavior, process step, control mechanism, or proof artifact.

### 9. Management-Level Picture Of The Day

When the source material mentions daily summaries, minus-one/minus-two control, or management visibility, extract the required "picture of the day":

- what happened today;
- which goals moved or stalled;
- which tasks and problems are open;
- who owns the next action;
- which departments affect the money path;
- what changed since yesterday;
- where tactical correction is needed now.

This layer is useful for owner, department head, office head, sales lead, and process owner messaging. It should be framed as faster tactical correction and less operational digging, not just prettier reporting.

### 10. CRM Boundary

If the source material admits that the product contains CRM-like elements, do not hide that. State the boundary:

- CRM-like elements are acceptable when they are required to make the revenue process controllable.
- Do not position the product as "just CRM" unless the user explicitly wants that category.
- Explain which CRM-like pieces are necessary: tasks, goals, problems, responsible owners, status, metrics, reporting, and control.
- The UTP must make the category distinction through the replaced process and business result, not through denial.

Keep the table specific. A row like "sales process -> inefficient -> AI helps -> faster -> revenue" is invalid. A row must name the real process step, the real pain/risk, the actual product behavior, and a measurable result.

## Review Rules

When reviewing a UTP draft, lead with concrete defects:

- claim is a slogan rather than a process replacement;
- current process is not decomposed;
- product behavior is unclear or overstated;
- no responsible owner or control point;
- current human-control dependency is ignored;
- management-control claim without tasks, goals, problems, status, or reporting mechanics;
- sales-only framing when the result depends on adjacent departments;
- objection handling answers the literal phrase but misses the real fear;
- CRM-like elements are either hidden or over-positioned instead of bounded by the process being replaced;
- metrics are missing, lagging, or not tied to the promised value;
- "AI" is used as proof instead of process, facts, and control;
- benefit is not linked to money, speed, quality, risk reduction, or management visibility;
- the UTP promises future capabilities as if they exist now.

If the draft passes, say what evidence is still missing: customer proof, baseline metrics, before/after timing, conversion impact, management adoption, or live product data.

## Copywriting Boundary

Requirements are not final copy. After requirements are accepted, copy can be generated for a specific artifact:

- landing hero;
- one-pager for owner or CEO;
- demo narrative for champion;
- cold outreach;
- sales deck slide;
- internal positioning statement.

When writing copy later, preserve the process-replacement chain and do not collapse it back into generic "save time" language.
